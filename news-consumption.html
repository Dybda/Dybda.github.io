Beyond the Major: How Exposure to Social Sciences and Humanities Shapes Political Information Behavior

Status: Under Review Methods: Stacked DiD / Administrative Records

Abstract

This paper asks whether it is what students study — not simply whether they attend university — that shapes how they seek out and consume political information. Using a longitudinal dataset spanning a politically turbulent period and linked to administrative records, we separate selection into higher education from the causal effects of course-level exposure to social sciences and humanities.

Full abstract available upon request.

Key Contributions

  • Causal identification of course-level effects on political media consumption, distinct from degree-level selection
  • Evidence of asymmetric effects across the ideological spectrum
  • Implications for debates on political diversity in news audiences and the role of liberal arts curricula

Data & Methods

  • Longitudinal student survey data linked to administrative course records (2019–2024)
  • Stacked difference-in-differences estimation
  • Outcomes: political news source preferences and information-seeking behaviour
