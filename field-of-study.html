Who Changes in College? Selection, Field of Study, and the Political Returns to Higher Education

Status: Working Paper Methods: Staggered DiD / Marginal Treatment Effects / Interactive Fixed Effects
Working paper — please do not cite without contacting the author. This is an actively developing draft. Estimates, framing, and conclusions may change in subsequent revisions. If you would like to discuss or reference the work, please email martin.dybdahl@eui.eu for the current version.

Abstract

Does higher education causally affect political behavior, or do the political choices of graduates simply reflect who was always going to enroll? This paper reframes the question as one of political returns, distinguishing engagement from partisan direction and asking how those returns are distributed, rather than trying to attain a perfectly exogenous measure of average effects. Extending the study of causal effects by comparing higher-education effects in the contrasting party systems of Germany and the United Kingdom. I ask how returns are distributed across predictors of university graduation, rather than abstracting from treatment selection as a theoretical nuisance to control for. Attainment shifts graduates toward the socially liberal and fiscally conservative parties, rather than the redistributive social democrats. However, this return is unevenly distributed, concentrating among the graduates most likely to attend in Germany and the least likely in the United Kingdom, dictated by electoral rules rather than educational differences. The credential alone produces similar shifts in every graduate; where they differ, it is due to their curriculum. The partisan returns belong to communicative-cultural fields, and the field of study governs whether their preferred party is taken to the ballot rather than whether it is formed.

Key Contributions

  • Reframes higher education's political effects as a distribution of returns rather than a single average treatment effect, treating selection into university as the analytic object rather than a nuisance to control away.
  • Cross-national comparison of how electoral rules condition the partisan return to a degree: the lean–vote inversion shows graduates in Germany leaning socialist but voting Green, and graduates in the UK voting Labour without leaning Labour, both reflecting the same underlying socially liberal preference under different electoral arithmetic.
  • Recovers a selection inversion across the two countries: the Greens shift in Germany is concentrated among graduates most likely to attend, while the Labour return in the UK is concentrated among graduates least likely to attend, both dictated by what electoral alternatives are available rather than by educational differences.
  • Disaggregates the credential from the curriculum: the credential alone produces uniform engagement gains and a socially liberal lean, but the partisan return at the ballot runs specifically through communicative-cultural and liberal-arts fields; STEM and economics graduates carry no detectable partisan effect.
  • Locates the field-of-study channel in vote anchoring rather than preference formation: low-CECT graduates form Greens preferences at comparable rates but translate them into ballots at one-fifth the rate of high-CECT graduates.
